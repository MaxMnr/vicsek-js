    • Vicsek Model: System of Self-Driven Particles

    Introduction

    The Vicsek model is a simple model with a novel type of dynamics introduced to investigate the emergence of self-ordered motion in systems of particles with biologically motivated interaction. In this model, particles are driven with a constant absolute velocity and at each time step, they assume the average direction of motion of the particles in their neighborhood with some random perturbation added. The model demonstrates a kinetic phase transition from no transport to finite net transport through spontaneous symmetry breaking of the rotational symmetry.

    Simulation

    Consider a box of size $\rho$ in which $N$ particles are placed. The $i$-th particle can be described by its position $\mathbf{x_i}$ and the direction of its velocity $\theta_i$, so that the velocity is computed by: \[ \mathbf{v_i}=v\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta_i \\ \sin\theta_i \end{pmatrix} \] with constant speed $v$.

    The angles of the particles are updated at each timestep to align with their neighbors. This means we update the angle with: \[\theta_i(t+\Delta t)=\langle\theta_i(t)\rangle_{r}+\eta\] where $\langle\theta_i(t)\rangle_{r}$ is the average direction of the velocities of the particles within a circle of radius $r$ around it, and $\eta$ is a noise term representing random perturbations, akin to a temperature-like variable.

    Now that we know how to update the direction of each particle, we define the position at the next timestep by: \[\mathbf{x_i}(t+1)=\mathbf{x_i}(t)+\mathbf{v}_i(t)\Delta t\]

    Order Parameter

    The average direction of motion, or the order parameter, is a crucial concept in the Vicsek model. It quantifies the degree of alignment of the particles. When the system transitions from disorder to order, the average direction changes from random orientations to a coherent movement in a single direction. This order parameter is defined as the normalized sum of the velocities of all particles: \[ \zeta = \frac{1}{Nv} \left| \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{v_i} \right| \] When the particles are aligned, \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}}\) approaches 1, indicating high order, while a value close to 0 indicates disorder.

    Results

    The Vicsek model exhibits four regimes based on varying values of density and noise:

    • For small densities and high noise, particles tend to form groups moving coherently in random directions.
    • At higher densities and high noise, particles move randomly with some correlation.
    • For higher density and low noise, motion becomes ordered on a macroscopic scale, and all particles tend to move in the same spontaneously selected direction.
    • At very high densities, the particles exhibit collective motion similar to a phase transition in equilibrium systems.

    These observations indicate that the model captures a transition from disordered to ordered motion, similar to the behavior of ferromagnetic systems, but in a nonequilibrium setting. This transition is continuous, as the average velocity scales with the noise intensity and particle density, showing critical behavior similar to equilibrium phase transitions.
